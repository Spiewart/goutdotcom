People who have had a gout flare will tell you: treatment is better than not. Treatment is divided into two categories: flare and prevention.

Flare Treatment About

Gout flares are the main driver of treatment. This is obvious due to how painful and disabling flares are. Flare treatment shortens the duration of flares, which are initially 5-7 days of excrutiating pain and disability. Unfortunately, they do not eliminate the flares entirely (you need preventative therapy for this).

There are generally 3 options for treatment of gout flares.

  1. NSAIDs: ibuprofen, naproxen, meloxicam, and Celebrex, among others.
    • NSAIDs are the most common gout flare treatment. They are effective, over the counter, safe, and cheap.
    • There are also prescription varietals that can be used for flares at the discretion of your doctor.
  2. Colchicine
    • Colchicine is as old as time and has been used to treat gout nearly as long.
    • Colchicine is only available by prescription in the US.
    • It can cause stomach upset.
  3. Steroids: prednisone, methylprednisolone.
    • Steroids are great for basically everything inflammatory.
    • They work very well for gout and can be given in high doses for difficult flares.
    • Steroids are associated with a litany of short- and long-term side effects.

Gout Prevention About

Long term management of gout is extremely effective. It can essentially eliminate gout flares. However it does involve continued use of medications

Luckily, the medications are extremely safe and well tolerated.

  1. Allopurinol is historically the most effective and commonly used therapy for chronic suppression of gout.
    • It is very safe both short- and long-term.
    • It has been on the market for over 50 years and has long been a cornerstone of gout management.
  2. Febuxostat is a newer cousin of allopurinol.
    • It has the same pharmacology of allopurinol and is thus very safe both short- and long-term.
    • It hasn't been around as long and there have been some concerns about the cardiovascular safety of its use. This topic is hotly debated.
  3. Probenecid is an older medication for gout.
    • It isn't used that much anymore because its generally not as effective or convenient as allopurinol or febuxotat.
    • Probenecid increases the amount of uric acid that is in urine, causing more excretion of uric acid via this route, but also probably predisposing individuals to uric acid kidney stones.
