NSAIDs (non-steroidal antiinflammatory drugs are the most common gout treatment. They are available over the counter, cheap, and are generally safe.

NSAIDs are likely recommended for you based off you being in a general good state of health. Certain medical conditions, such as chronic kidney disease (CKD), a history of gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding, heart attack(s), stroke(s), or needing to be on a blood thinner are generally contraindications to use of NSAIDs.

Some particularly bad gout flares are resistant to treatment with NSAIDs and are better treated with prescription medications and or joint injections. These flares typically involve multiple joints, often large weight-bearing joints such as the ankle or knee, and are very disabling.

{% elif object.decision_aid == "steroids" %}

Prednisone and methylprednisolone are corticosteroids and are some of the most effective antiinflammatories we have. They are most commonly used in gout when a flare is particularly bad or a patient has medical reasons not to use other therapies.

They can have some side effects with both short- and long-term use and thus are prescription only.

With short-term use, such as for a gout flare, they can cause anxiety, insomnia, hunger, weight gain, elevated blood sugars (hyperglycemia).

Colchicine is the oldest still available treatment for gout flares. You most likely were recommended it because you have healthy kidneys but a contraindication to NSAIDs. It is most effective when started within the first 36 hours of a flare.
