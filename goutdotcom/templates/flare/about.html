Gout flares are painful and disabling. They are caused by uric acid build up in one or more joints where it causes inflammation. Inflammation causes pain, redness, swelling, and warmth over the joint(s).

Basics

Pain

The cardinal symptom of gout flares is pain. The pain is typically characterized as starting "mild" as "an itch" or "a tickle" before bedtime. The next morning, the pain will have become severe. It will sometimes wake the patient from sleep before their usual waking hour. At this point, the pain is so severe that sufferers often believe they have broken their bone. It grows more violent and even light touch, such as that imparted by wearing a sock or setting a sheet down, will cause severe distress.

Gout flares thus start quite suddenly and reach maximal intensity within hours. Flares have a predilection for starting in the evening or early morning, when the joint is coolest.

Inflammation

There are typically signs of inflammation in the involved joint. Redness (erythema or rubor) of the skin overlying the joint is commonly observed on visual inspection. The joint and overlying skin are often warm to the touch (calor). In addition to pain (dolor), the joint is often quite swollen (tumor).

Often, sufferers will have a low-grade fever from the systemic effects of such severe localized inflammation.

It is not uncommon for gout to be misdiagnosed as an infection of the skin (cellulitis) due to the "angry" appearance of the skin overlying the affected joint. This can result in unnecessary exposure to antibiotics and/or other testing or treatments. Even more problematic is when gout is misdiagnosed as an infected joint (septic arthritis). This often means an unnecessary surgery to clean out the joint. This causes a delay in the accurate diagnosis as well as a non-trivial recovery time in addition to the risk of surgical complications.

Location

Gout has a predilection for the lower extremities when it initially begins to affect an individual. The big toe is the most common site and is frequently the site of a sufferer's first flare. When gout effects the big toe, it is termed podagra. Gout also commonly occurs in the midfoot, ankle and knees.

Early on in the course of an individual's gout, it is usually monoarticular (affects only one joint at a time). As the disease progresses, it can involve multiple joints at the same time (polyarticular). It can also start to involve more atypical sites such as those found in the upper extremities and proximal lower extremities. Advanced gout has a predilection for the wrist and elbow. It can also involve the fingers.

Gout rarely involves the hips, back/spine, and shoulders.

Duration

Gout typically lasts 3-10 days and resolves on its own. This is not to diminish the severe disability it causes. If the disease progresses, flares occur more and more frequently, involve more joints, and can last longer.

Biology

Inflammation

The involvement of uric acid in human biology has been appreciated in a crude, by modern standards, fashion for hundreds of years (1). The first and landmark paper describing urate crystals in gouty joints (2) was published by McCarty et al in 1961. It was subsequently recognized that uric acid can cause inflammation in a variety of mammalian tissues. Modern molecular biology allowed us to understand that uric acid causes inflammation by a very old (genetically) family of proteins called the inflammasome (3).
