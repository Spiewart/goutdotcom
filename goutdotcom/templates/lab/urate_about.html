Uric acid is the most well recognized and important lab for the understanding and management of gout. Elevated levels of uric acid in the blood cause it to deposit in the joints where it can cause inflammation (gout attacks). Uric acid is consumed in our diet as well as produced by our body's normal functions. It is disposed of by the kidneys.

Biology

Uric acid in the human body is derived from two sources:

  1. Dietary consumption in the form of purines. Eating purine-rich foods are associated with elevated levels of blood uric acid (1).
  2. Bodily production as a part of normal cellular metabolism.

Diet

Gout was historically termed the "disease of kings" due to its afflicting only the wealthy societal elite. When this is appreciated in the context of gout exacerbations occurring in close temporal relation to dietary overindulgence, it is clear diet plays a strong role in gout predilection. We now know that gout is driven by uric acid and that dietary factors leading to increased urate load drive gout. The nobility's diet in less modern times was more plentiful and contained more gout-causing items, such as wine, organ meats, shellfish, and red meat.

Several different types of foods drive urate production, however they all drive urate production via purines. Alcohol receives the most notoriety as a gout precipitant. Alcohol, even in moderate quantities increases risk of gout, particularly in the first 24 hours after consumption. The risk goes up with each subsequent drink (2). Beer is the most risky, followed by spirits, whereas moderate wine consumption does not increase risk (3). This simply reflects the relative purine levels in each type of beverage

In line with gout being the "disease of kings", purine-rich foods drive the body's production of urate. Commonly recognized examples are some seafoods, red meat, and organ meats.

  • Seafoods: anchovies, sardines, tuna, and shellfish (mussels, scallops, shrimp, etc.)
  • Red meat: beef, lamb, and pork (is this really a red meat?!)
  • Organ meats: liver, kidney, sweetbread

The final important dietary factor to consider is fructose. While commonly described as "fruit-based", fructose has health consequences in that it is very sweet and very cheap and thus is easily consumed in excess. Fructose consumption and metabolism causes the body to produce purines and subsequently uric acid.

In Gout

In individuals who have had gout previously, consumption of purine-rich foods is associated with gout flares (4).
